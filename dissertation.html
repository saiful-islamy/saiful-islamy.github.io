The Problem

700 million women locked out of the financial system

Despite decades of financial inclusion efforts, a persistent gender gap remains. Globally, women are significantly less likely than men to hold a bank account, receive wages formally, save, or access credit. This isn't just unfair — it traps families in poverty cycles and limits entire economies.

Mobile banking promised a solution. A phone, an agent network, and a SIM card could theoretically replace a branch and bypass the social barriers — husband's permission, distance, documentation — that kept women out. But did it actually work? That's what this research set out to measure.

"The question wasn't whether mobile banking existed. It was whether it changed anything for the women who had it."

Methodology

How I approached the analysis

01
Data sourcing & cleaning
Downloaded female-only microdata from the World Bank Global Findex 2021 survey — 71,000+ respondents across 144 countries. Cleaned missing values, standardised country-level variables (female labour force participation, internet penetration, mobile agent density), and engineered binary outcome variables for six financial behaviours.
02
Theoretical framework
Grounded the analysis in Kabeer's (1999) Resources-Agency-Achievements empowerment framework — treating mobile account ownership as the resource, and the six financial outcomes (wages, savings, borrowing, emergency funds, G2P transfers, mobile payments) as measurable achievements.
03
Logistic regression modelling
Built six separate logistic regression models — one per financial outcome — controlling for age, education, income quintile, employment, and country-level contextual variables. Calculated Average Marginal Effects (AME) to quantify the percentage-point impact of mobile account ownership on each outcome.
04
Machine learning validation
Trained and compared three ML models — Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost — using 70/30 train-test split with cross-validation. XGBoost achieved the best performance with AUC 0.787, confirming the predictive power of mobile account ownership on savings behaviour. Feature importance analysis confirmed mobile account as the strongest predictor across all models.
05
Policy recommendations
Translated findings into actionable recommendations for policymakers, financial institutions, and NGOs — focusing on agent network density, female labour force participation, and digital infrastructure as the key enablers of impact.

Key Findings

What the data showed

Mobile account ownership had a statistically significant positive effect on all six financial outcomes studied. The strongest effects were on wage receipt (2.9x) and emergency fund access (+26.2pp) — both directly relevant to household financial resilience.

Context mattered. Effects were stronger in countries with higher female labour force participation, better digital infrastructure, and denser mobile agent networks. This shows that mobile banking alone isn't enough — the enabling environment determines the impact.

Age-related disparities and weaker borrowing effects highlighted that empowerment through digital finance is conditional, not automatic. Older women and those in low-connectivity regions saw smaller gains.

Why this matters for banking & fintech roles

This research isn't just academic. Understanding how financial access changes real behaviour — savings patterns, wage routing, credit uptake — is exactly what product teams, risk analysts, and fraud detection units in fintech need to build better systems.

The methodology — cleaning messy real-world survey data, building and comparing multiple ML models, quantifying marginal effects, translating findings for non-technical stakeholders — mirrors the day-to-day work of a data or business analyst in a financial institution.

The findings directly inform questions like: Who should we target for financial products? What predicts savings behaviour? How do we measure financial inclusion impact? These are live questions at every bank and fintech operating in emerging markets.
